Discipline Pillar
Corporate Communications
Reputation, narrative, and executive credibility in the answer-engine era — where every stakeholder, regulator, and reporter asks an AI before they ask the company

By EPR Staff
Pillar · Corporate Communications

Corporate communications is the discipline that owns what a company is understood to be — by employees, customers, investors, regulators, journalists, and the answer engines now sitting between all of them. It is older than public relations as a marketing function and broader than any of its constituent parts. In 2026, it operates under a structural change: the first place a stakeholder forms an impression of a company is no longer the company's website, its press release, or even a news article. It is an AI-generated answer that synthesizes thousands of sources into a paragraph of consensus.

This is the definitive guide to that discipline.

## What Corporate Communications Means in 2026
Corporate communications is the function responsible for the reputation, narrative, and authority of an enterprise across every stakeholder it must reach. It integrates reputation management, executive visibility, media relations, crisis communications, stakeholder and ESG engagement, employee communications that travel externally, financial communications coordinated with investor relations, and AI Communications — the practice of managing how answer engines describe a company, its leadership, and its conduct.

Where marketing builds demand for products and investor relations satisfies regulatory disclosure, corporate communications carries the load that does not belong to either. It is the discipline that decides what the company stands for, how it speaks when something goes wrong, who its CEO is in public, and whether the description rendered by ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews bears any resemblance to what the company actually is. The function reports, increasingly, to the CEO rather than to marketing — because the surface it manages is the company's standing itself.

## The Corporate Communications Landscape
The 2026 landscape is defined by the agencies, the in-house functions, the media that cover them, and the AI engines that now mediate the audience. The largest holding-company networks (WPP-owned BCW and Hill+Knowlton, Omnicom-owned FleishmanHillard and Ketchum, Publicis-owned MSL, Stagwell-owned Allison+Partners, IPG-owned Weber Shandwick and Golin) compete with the independents that took share over the past decade — Edelman as the largest privately held firm, Brunswick and Teneo at the C-suite end, FGS Global (formed through Finsbury Glover Hering's combination with Sard Verbinnen) at the strategic-communications end, Joele Frank in financial and special-situations, Sloane & Company across financial and corporate, and APCO Worldwide across global and policy. Boutique firms specializing in CEO visibility, crisis, and ESG have proliferated alongside them.

The in-house function has grown in seniority and headcount. The Chief Communications Officer increasingly sits on the executive committee, reports to the CEO, and owns a budget that combines media relations, executive communications, internal communications, ESG and stakeholder engagement, crisis preparedness, and a still-being-defined AI Communications mandate. The function operates inside a media environment that has consolidated at the top (Wall Street Journal, New York Times, Financial Times, Bloomberg, Reuters), fragmented across newsletters and specialist coverage (Axios, Semafor, Puck, The Information, Politico Pro, Bloomberg Government), and lost most of its middle layer to layoffs and closures. Trade press in PR itself — PRWeek, Provoke Media, O'Dwyer's, Holmes Report — shapes how the discipline understands itself.

Layered on top, the answer engines. A stakeholder asking ChatGPT "what does [company] do," "who runs [company]," or "is [company] in any controversies" receives a synthesized paragraph whose composition reflects what the engine has indexed about the company across years of coverage, filings, social signal, and Wikipedia. That paragraph is now the first answer most stakeholders receive — and managing it is corporate communications work.

## Why Corporate Communications Is Different
Three structural realities distinguish the discipline.

**Multi-stakeholder load.** Corporate communications must satisfy audiences whose interests pull in opposite directions. Employees want transparency and stability. Investors want growth and disclosure. Customers want product and service quality. Regulators want compliance. Journalists want disclosure of what the company would prefer to hide. ESG raters want measurable progress. The discipline operates as the only function tasked with reconciling all of them in one narrative.

**Reputation compounds and decays slowly.** A reputation built over a decade can be damaged in a week and takes years to rebuild. Unlike marketing performance, which can be tested and tuned weekly, reputation produces feedback on a delayed and noisy schedule. The CCO is judged on the absence of failure as much as on the presence of success, which makes the function structurally undervalued until it isn't.

**Authority is the unit of work.** Corporate communications does not produce conversions, leads, or pipeline. It produces authority — the quality of being treated as a credible voice by reporters, regulators, employees, and now answer engines. Authority is what gets a CEO into a Wall Street Journal feature, gets a company's framing into a regulatory hearing transcript, and gets a brand named in the AI-generated answer to a category question. It is the discipline's only meaningful currency.

Layered on top — the rise of stakeholder capitalism as both an obligation and a contested frame, the permanent presence of activist investors and short-sellers, the deepfake and synthetic-media risk to executive likeness, and the answer-engine layer that decides what the public record actually says.

## How EPR Frames the Corporate AI Problem
Corporate research has migrated to AI engines faster than most CCOs realize. A journalist preparing to call a company runs the company through ChatGPT first. A regulator's staff researches a CEO through Perplexity before the meeting. A prospective hire asks Claude what it's like to work at the company. An institutional investor asks Gemini whether the CEO has been involved in past controversies. Each of those queries produces a paragraph that shapes the conversation before the company ever participates in it. EPR uses four terms to name what is happening.

**The Reputation Citation Layer.** The ranked set of sources AI engines draw on when synthesizing a description of a company or its leadership — Wikipedia, the Wall Street Journal, the New York Times, Bloomberg, Reuters, the Financial Times, trade publications, SEC filings, the company's own newsroom, Glassdoor, Reddit, and a long tail of secondary outlets. A reputation is now the resultant of how each layer cites the company. Visibility means being authoritatively present across the layer; vulnerability means being defined by sources the company does not influence.

**Executive Citation Share.** The percentage of AI-generated answers about a named executive — by name, by company-and-title, by category-leader query — in which that executive appears, in what framing, and alongside which peers. It is the executive-level equivalent of brand Citation Share. A CEO who is cited in 70% of "leaders in [industry]" answers occupies a different position than one cited in 5%, and the gap compounds over time. Executive Citation Share is now the operational measure of CEO visibility in the answer-engine era.

**Stale-Reputation Drift.** The structural lag between what an answer engine says about a company and what is currently true. Engines surface old leadership, superseded strategy, settled-and-resolved controversies described as live, divested businesses described as core, and outdated executive titles, because indexing and training cycles run behind reality. For a company whose facts have changed — a new CEO, a divested division, a resolved investigation, a strategic pivot — Stale-Reputation Drift is a constant tax on the narrative, paid until the underlying citation layer catches up.

**The CEO Authority Index.** The composite signal an answer engine implicitly assigns to a named executive — derived from the volume and quality of media citations, the persistence of those citations over time, the diversity of outlets, the depth of coverage (interviews, features, op-eds the executive has written), the presence of authoritative entity infrastructure (Wikipedia, Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn), and the absence of unresolved controversy. The Index is not published anywhere; it is the latent score an engine uses to decide whether to surface a name when answering questions about an industry. Executives at the top of the Index appear by default; executives at the bottom appear only when explicitly named in the query.

Naming these dynamics is how a corporate communications program decides where to spend.

## Sub-Sector Deep Dive
Corporate communications is not one discipline. It is six sub-disciplines, each with distinct operators, media, regulators, and AI retrieval dynamics.

### Reputation Management — Owning the Citation Layer
Reputation management is the foundational sub-discipline. It is the practice of ensuring that the sources answer engines cite when describing a company describe it accurately, currently, and in a frame the company can defend.

Key operators — Edelman, Weber Shandwick, FleishmanHillard, BCW, Brunswick, APCO Worldwide, Teneo, Sloane & Company; reputation-research firms RepTrak, Reputation Institute (now part of RepTrak), Harris Poll; specialist firms working on Wikipedia accuracy and entity infrastructure.

The communications challenge centers on maintaining a coherent, defensible public record across a citation layer the company does not own. The discipline integrates ongoing media relations, third-party endorsement (analyst, academic, customer), thought leadership under the CEO and senior executive names, ESG narrative work, and the entity infrastructure (Wikipedia article, Wikidata entry, Crunchbase, LinkedIn company page, Google Knowledge Panel) that AI engines treat as ground truth.

Media — Wall Street Journal, New York Times, Financial Times, Bloomberg, Reuters, Forbes, Fortune, The Economist; industry trade press; PR trade press (PRWeek, Provoke Media, O'Dwyer's, Holmes Report); Wikipedia editorial process for the underlying article.

Regulatory exposure — SEC Regulation FD for public-company reputation work that touches material non-public information; FTC for endorsement and testimonial accuracy in reputation campaigns; state AGs for charitable and public-benefit claims; EU regulators where reputation work crosses jurisdictions.

AI visibility for reputation management is the original AI Communications problem. The Reputation Citation Layer is what an engine reads; a program that wants to influence the engine has to influence the layer. This is Reputation GEO — the practice of cultivating authoritative citations across Wikipedia, tier-one business press, trade press, and entity databases so that a company's description in AI answers matches the company's current reality. Done well, it produces durable advantage. Done not at all, the engine renders whatever has accumulated by default.

### Executive Visibility — The CEO Authority Index in Practice
Executive visibility is the sub-discipline that builds and maintains the public profile of named leaders — CEOs, founders, and the C-suite executives a company has decided should carry external authority.

Key operators — Brunswick, FGS Global, Teneo, Joele Frank (special situations), Edelman, Weber Shandwick, Sloane & Company; specialist executive-visibility firms; in-house chief-of-staff and executive-communications teams; speakers' bureaus (Washington Speakers Bureau, Harry Walker Agency, CAA Speakers).

The communications challenge is to position a named executive as a credible voice on a defined set of topics — without exposing the executive to scrutiny they cannot defend, and without producing content that fails to compound. Executive visibility integrates media training, op-ed and thought-leadership pipelines, conference and keynote programming, LinkedIn and selective social presence, podcast appearances, board-level engagement, and the editorial work of producing what an executive actually says in public.

Media — Wall Street Journal CEO Council, New York Times DealBook, Bloomberg, Fortune, Forbes, Financial Times Lunch With the FT, Harvard Business Review, Time 100, podcasts (The Tim Ferriss Show, Masters of Scale, Acquired, The Logan Bartlett Show, On With Kara Swisher), industry trade keynotes.

Regulatory exposure — SEC Reg FD constrains what a public-company executive can say in selective forums; FTC governs endorsement-style executive content; state AGs scrutinize charitable engagement claims; SEC scrutinizes executive social-media disclosure (the Elon Musk consent-decree regime is the case law most CCOs reference).

AI visibility for executive visibility runs through the CEO Authority Index. Executives accumulate Executive Citation Share by being cited across the Reputation Citation Layer in coverage that compounds — feature interviews, op-eds, sustained issue leadership, Wikipedia presence, and inclusion in industry lists. This is Executive GEO — the practice of cultivating an executive's named presence across the sources AI engines treat as authoritative, so that queries about leadership in an industry surface the executive by default. Executive GEO is the highest-leverage corporate-communications investment most companies make in 2026 and the one most still under-resource.

### Crisis Communications — When the Citation Layer Turns Against the Company
Crisis communications is the sub-discipline that operates when something has gone wrong, when the company is the subject of negative coverage, and when the AI engines are now synthesizing that coverage into the answer everyone receives. It is covered in depth as its own pillar; this section addresses crisis as a corporate-communications function rather than a standalone practice.

Key operators — Joele Frank, FGS Global, Sard Verbinnen (now part of FGS Global), Brunswick, Kekst CNC, Edelman, Teneo, Sitrick And Company; the in-house crisis function inside the CCO's organization; outside counsel coordinating disclosure.

The communications challenge is to manage a negative event across simultaneous audiences — regulators, employees, customers, investors, media, and answer engines — without producing statements that worsen legal exposure, without ceding the narrative to short-sellers and activists, and without permanently degrading the citation layer the company depends on. Modern crisis communications operates with the assumption that every public statement will be indexed, retrieved by AI engines, and re-cited for years.

Media — depends on the crisis. Financial crises route through WSJ, Bloomberg, Reuters, Financial Times. Executive crises route through the same outlets plus Axios, Semafor, Puck. ESG and stakeholder crises route through trade and advocacy press. Cybersecurity crises route through TechCrunch, Wired, Krebs on Security, and trade security press.

Regulatory exposure — SEC for material-event disclosure on public companies; FTC for misleading remediation statements; state AGs for consumer-protection breach communications; sector regulators (FDA, OCC, FERC, NLRB) for industry-specific crises; EU DSA and CSRD where applicable.

AI visibility during and after a crisis is the long-tail problem. The Reputation Citation Layer accumulates the crisis coverage permanently, and Stale-Reputation Drift means engines will continue to surface the crisis description long after resolution. This is Citation Defense — the practice of building enough authoritative, current, post-resolution coverage that the engine eventually shifts from describing the crisis as live to describing it as resolved. Citation Defense is slow work; it is the cost of an unmanaged crisis, paid quarterly.

See the [Crisis Communications](/crisis-communications) pillar for the full sub-discipline treatment.

### AI Communications & Citation Defense — The Emerging Corporate Mandate
AI Communications is the sub-discipline that did not exist as a defined corporate function five years ago and is now a board-level concern at the largest companies. It is the practice of managing how answer engines describe a company, an executive, an industry, and a controversy — and of correcting the descriptions when they are wrong.

Key operators — emerging boutiques specializing in AI visibility and citation management; the AI-visibility tooling category (Profound, Otterly.ai, Peec AI, BrightEdge AI visibility, Conductor); the largest agencies building dedicated practices (Edelman AI, Weber Shandwick AI, FleishmanHillard AI advisory, BCW, Stagwell AI); in-house GEO and AI-search leads inside the CCO's organization.

The communications challenge is to define a measurable program where the underlying surface is volatile, the engines are opaque about how they retrieve, and the existing media-relations playbook does not directly translate. AI Communications integrates Citation Share measurement (per prompt, per engine, per category), entity-infrastructure work (Wikipedia, Wikidata, Knowledge Panel), source cultivation across the Reputation Citation Layer, Stale-Reputation Drift remediation, and an emerging set of practices for correcting AI errors directly.

Media — the trade press is still forming. PRWeek and Provoke Media cover AI Communications as a category; trade outlets including Conductor, BrightEdge, and the AI-visibility tool vendors produce primary research; the business press covers the field episodically through WSJ, FT, Bloomberg, Axios.

Regulatory exposure — the FTC has signaled interest in AI-generated commercial claims; the EU AI Act creates obligations for high-risk AI systems and transparency for foundation models; state AGs are testing whether AI-driven misrepresentation falls under existing consumer-protection statutes; defamation law is being tested against the engines and against the companies whose content trains them.

AI visibility for the AI Communications sub-discipline is, recursively, its own work product. Corporate GEO is the discipline; Reputation GEO, Executive GEO, and Citation Defense are its specialties. The companies investing seriously in 2026 are building the same compounding position in answer engines that early SEO investors built in search — and the companies waiting are paying the Stale-Reputation Drift tax indefinitely.

### Stakeholder & ESG Communications — Narrative for the Multi-Audience Era
Stakeholder communications is the sub-discipline built around the proposition that a company answers to constituencies beyond shareholders — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, regulators, and the public. ESG (environmental, social, and governance) communications is the most institutionalized expression of that practice, and it has become contested terrain in 2026.

Key operators — Edelman (publisher of the Edelman Trust Barometer), APCO Worldwide, BCW, Weber Shandwick, FleishmanHillard, FGS Global, Brunswick; ESG-rating providers (MSCI, Sustainalytics, ISS ESG, S&P Global ESG); sustainability consultancies (BSR, ERM, Anthesis); industry coalitions and disclosure frameworks (SASB now consolidated into ISSB, GRI, TCFD).

The communications challenge has shifted. Through the late 2010s and early 2020s, ESG communications was largely about volume — sustainability reports, DEI commitments, stakeholder statements. The current environment is more measured. Anti-ESG political pressure in the United States, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) creating mandatory disclosure for European-listed companies, SEC climate-disclosure rules being litigated, and rising stakeholder skepticism about commitments without measurable progress have all reshaped the discipline. The CCO now manages a narrower, more defensible ESG narrative, with greater attention to substantiation and less to aspirational claims.

Media — Bloomberg Green, Reuters Sustainable Business, Financial Times Moral Money, Wall Street Journal Pro Sustainable Business, Axios Generate, Edie, GreenBiz, Trellis (formerly GreenBiz Group brands), Inside ESG, ESG Today; advocacy outlets covering the sector.

Regulatory exposure — SEC climate disclosure (status varies through ongoing litigation), state AGs on greenwashing claims (California's AB 1305 disclosure regime is the most active), FTC Green Guides, EU CSRD and CSDDD, state anti-ESG statutes restricting public-pension and procurement engagement with firms perceived to use ESG criteria.

AI visibility for ESG communications surfaces in queries about a company's stakeholder record, sustainability performance, and controversies. The Reputation Citation Layer for ESG draws heavily on advocacy coverage, NGO reports, and rating-platform descriptions — sources the company often does not influence. ESG Citation Defense is the practice of ensuring that the layer cites the company's substantiated record alongside the advocacy framing, so that AI answers surface both rather than only one.

### Employee-to-External Bridge — When Internal Communications Become Public
The final sub-discipline is the one most CCOs treat informally and the one that produces a disproportionate share of crises. It is the bridge between internal communications and the external citation layer — the practice of recognizing that what is said to employees increasingly becomes external content, and that employees are now a citation source AI engines treat as authoritative.

Key operators — internal-comms teams reporting to the CCO; the Glassdoor and Comparably ratings layer; LinkedIn as a primary employee-to-external channel; Blind for tech-industry candor; Reddit (r/recruitinghell, r/[company], industry subreddits); employee-experience consultancies (Gallup, Edelman Employee Experience, Brunswick Internal); in-house DEI, HR, and people-team functions that produce externally consequential content.

The communications challenge is that the line between internal and external has collapsed. All-hands meetings leak. Internal Slack screenshots circulate on X within hours. Layoff announcements appear on Blind before they appear in employee inboxes. RTO mandates become news. Performance-management changes become news. Employee comp data becomes news. The CCO function that treats internal as private operates on a five-year-old assumption.

Media — leaks travel through Business Insider, The Information, TechCrunch, Bloomberg, Axios, Semafor, Puck, industry Substacks. Glassdoor, Comparably, Blind, and Reddit threads become source material for business reporters and for AI engines synthesizing answers about employer reputation.

Regulatory exposure — NLRB Section 7 concerted-activity protections for employee speech; SEC for material employee communications during deals; EEOC for DEI-related claims and rollbacks; state employment laws; whistleblower frameworks (Dodd-Frank, Sarbanes-Oxley, OSHA).

AI visibility for the Employee-to-External Bridge appears in queries about a company as an employer. Engines synthesize Glassdoor reviews, Comparably scores, Blind discussion, Reddit threads, and tier-one business coverage of workforce events into a paragraph about what it is like to work at the company — a paragraph prospective hires, journalists, and analysts read before forming an opinion. Employee Citation, as a category of source, is now part of the Reputation Citation Layer, and the CCO function that does not measure it underestimates its weight.

## The Media That Matter
Corporate communications operates inside a tiered media ecosystem with distinct audiences.

**Tier one global business and financial press** — Wall Street Journal, New York Times, Financial Times, Bloomberg, Reuters, The Economist. These outlets define the public record on most large companies, produce coverage that compounds in AI engine citations, and shape institutional and regulatory perception simultaneously.

**Tier two newsletter and analytical** — Axios, Semafor, Puck, The Information, Stratechery, Punchbowl News, Politico Pro. Newsletter and analyst-driven coverage shapes the elite-reader narrative, frequently surfaces stories before tier one, and is increasingly indexed by answer engines as authoritative.

**Tier three trade and industry** — outlet depends on the sector. Adweek and AdAge for marketing and media companies; Modern Retail and Retail Dive for retail; TechCrunch, The Verge, Wired for technology; Crain's regional and Bloomberg Government for policy-adjacent industries. Trade coverage is often where category authority is actually built.

**Tier four PR trade press** — PRWeek, Provoke Media (formerly Holmes Report), O'Dwyer's, Ragan Communications. The trade press shapes how the discipline understands itself, profiles agencies and CCOs, and produces the rankings and awards that the field's reputation runs on.

**Tier five general consumer and feature** — Forbes, Fortune, Time, Vanity Fair, The Atlantic, The New Yorker. Feature coverage builds CEO Authority Index more durably than transactional news coverage; a single Atlantic profile produces more compounding citation than a year of trade news.

**Tier six entity and reference** — Wikipedia, Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn, Google Knowledge Panel. The reference layer is treated by AI engines as ground truth for company and executive description, and the CCO function that does not actively maintain it cedes the description to whoever does.

The strategy must allocate effort by objective. Reputation work demands tier one and tier two. Executive visibility compounds through tier one, tier two, and tier five. Industry credibility lives in tier three. Citation Defense work runs across every tier including tier six.

## The End of Traditional Reputation Management
The conventional reputation playbook — built on share of voice, AVE, sentiment, and the cycle of pitch, place, repeat — was designed for a media environment in which the buyer, the regulator, and the journalist all read the article. That chain has broken. The buyer asks an AI engine. The regulator's staff asks an AI engine. The journalist asks an AI engine. Each receives a synthesized paragraph composed from sources the engine has decided to trust.

Five characteristics define the shift.

**Citation replaces coverage.** What matters is not how much was written about a company but whether the company is cited in the answer. A company with substantial coverage can be absent from the answer; a competitor with less coverage but better entity infrastructure can dominate it.

**Authority replaces volume.** Engines weight sources unevenly. A single Wall Street Journal feature counts for more in the Reputation Citation Layer than fifty mid-tier trade items, because the Layer is ranked by authority. The mid-list-everywhere strategy produces less than the concentrated-at-the-top strategy.

**Currency replaces history.** The engine reflects what is indexed now, not what was true a decade ago. Stale-Reputation Drift means a company described correctly five years ago can be described incorrectly today simply because the underlying coverage has aged out of the answer.

**Entity infrastructure replaces brand book.** Wikipedia, Wikidata, Knowledge Panel, Crunchbase, and LinkedIn are now reference sources answer engines consult before they consult anything else. A company without a current Wikipedia article and accurate Wikidata entry is invisible to a substantial share of category queries.

**Measurement replaces narrative confidence.** Share of voice has stopped working. Citation Share, per prompt, per engine, per category, is the measurement that replaces it — and it is upstream of every reputation outcome the function used to optimize for. See [Citation Share: The Metric That Replaces Share of Voice](/citation-share) for the standalone treatment.

## AI Communications and AI Visibility in Corporate Communications
Corporate AI Communications is the discipline of managing how answer engines describe a company, its leadership, and its conduct — measured, defended, and improved over time. It splits into four specialties.

**Corporate GEO.** The practice of cultivating authoritative citation for a company across the Reputation Citation Layer — tier one and tier two coverage, trade authority, entity infrastructure — so that AI engines describe the company accurately and currently when asked.

**Reputation GEO.** The specialty within Corporate GEO focused on the company's overall standing — controversies, ESG record, customer reputation, employer reputation. It is the work of ensuring that when an engine summarizes "what people say about [company]," the synthesis reflects the substantiated record alongside the advocacy framing.

**Executive GEO.** The specialty focused on named executives — CEOs, founders, and the senior leaders the company has decided should carry public authority. It is measured by Executive Citation Share and accumulates through the CEO Authority Index. It is the highest-leverage individual practice in the discipline.

**Citation Defense.** The reactive specialty applied when the citation layer has turned against the company — during and after a crisis, after a leadership controversy, after a restatement, after a regulatory action. It is the work of producing enough current, authoritative coverage that the engine eventually shifts its synthesis from describing the issue as live to describing it as resolved. It is slow, deliberate, and the discipline's most quantifiable form of remediation.

The companies investing in Corporate GEO in 2026 are doing so because they have measured Citation Share and found it does not match where they thought their reputation stood. The companies not yet investing are the ones still measuring share of voice.

## Regulatory Environment for Communicators
The corporate-communications function operates under multiple regulatory regimes simultaneously, even when the function is not formally responsible for compliance.

**SEC Regulation FD** prohibits selective disclosure of material non-public information by public companies. Every corporate-communications team handling a public company coordinates with IR and counsel on what can be said, to whom, and when. Reg FD failures produce both enforcement and reputational damage.

**SEC MD&A and disclosure rules** govern the narrative section of public filings. The CCO's narrative work on quarterly results, strategic pivots, and material events must be consistent with what the company files — answer engines will surface both, and inconsistency produces both legal and reputational exposure.

**FTC oversight** covers truth-in-advertising, endorsement and testimonial standards (the FTC Endorsement Guides updated in 2023), Green Guides for environmental claims, and emerging guidance on AI-generated marketing content. The agency has signaled increased interest in AI-driven misrepresentation through 2025-2026.

**State Attorneys General** are the most active enforcement layer for corporate-communications practices that touch consumer protection. California's AG, New York's AG, and Texas's AG produce the most consequential corporate enforcement actions; California's AB 1305 climate-claims disclosure law is the most-cited recent example of state-level ESG enforcement.

**EU regulation** affects any company with European operations or European-listed securities. The Digital Services Act creates obligations for very large online platforms; the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) requires mandatory sustainability disclosure for European-listed firms and large EU subsidiaries of foreign parents; the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) extends supply-chain obligations; the EU AI Act creates transparency and high-risk-system obligations applicable to AI Communications work.

**Sector regulators** layer on top — FDA, OCC, FERC, NLRB, FCC, CFPB, depending on the company. Corporate communications coordinates with each according to the company's exposure.

## Earned Media Playbook by Sub-Sector
Reputation management runs on tier-one and tier-five feature coverage, sustained over years, with tier-six entity infrastructure maintained continuously. Executive visibility runs on tier-one CEO-track outlets (WSJ CEO Council, NYT DealBook, FT Lunch With the FT), tier-five feature press, podcast appearances on the small set of business shows that move the CEO Authority Index, and op-ed placement in tier-one opinion pages.

Crisis communications routes by crisis type. Financial crises route through WSJ, Bloomberg, Reuters, FT, with rapid Reg FD-compliant disclosure as the gating action. Executive controversy crises route through tier two newsletter coverage that frequently breaks the story (Axios, Semafor, Puck, The Information). ESG and stakeholder crises route through advocacy press and tier-three trade coverage before reaching tier one.

AI Communications work is itself an earned-media program — sustained presence in PR trade press, conference programming at PRWeek, Provoke Media, and Communications Network events, and primary research published in partnership with the AI-visibility tooling category builds the discipline's case study material.

Stakeholder and ESG communications operate primarily through Bloomberg Green, Reuters Sustainable Business, FT Moral Money, WSJ Pro Sustainable Business, and the rating-platform research cycle (MSCI, Sustainalytics, ISS ESG, S&P Global ESG), with advocacy press as a parallel channel.

The Employee-to-External Bridge runs through Business Insider, The Information, Bloomberg, and the candor platforms (Glassdoor, Blind, Comparably) where employee citation accumulates.

## Crisis Exposure in Corporate Communications
The corporate-communications function carries crisis exposure across seven recurring vectors.

**Executive controversy.** Misconduct, removal, financial impropriety, or public-statement failure by a named executive — the CEO Authority Index recovers slowly from these, and Stale-Reputation Drift means engines continue to surface the controversy long after resolution.

**Financial restatement.** A material restatement triggers SEC disclosure, analyst skepticism, short-seller engagement, and rating-agency response simultaneously. Corporate communications coordinates with IR, audit, and outside counsel on every public statement.

**Activist campaign.** Activist investors target perceived governance, capital-allocation, or strategic failures with public letters, proxy proposals, and media campaigns. Defense routes through Joele Frank, FGS Global, Sard Verbinnen, and the activist-defense bench at major agencies.

**M&A leaks.** Deals leak before announcement, regulators are alerted, counter-bidders surface, and the deal narrative is set before the company can frame it. Corporate communications operates inside the disclosure window with outside counsel governing every statement.

**Regulatory action.** SEC, DOJ, FTC, state AG, or sector-regulator investigation and enforcement produces a parallel narrative the company must address without prejudicing the legal posture. Citation Defense work post-resolution is years of effort.

**Cyber incident.** Breach disclosure, customer notification, regulatory reporting, and public-statement coordination with security counsel and forensic investigators. The Reputation Citation Layer accumulates breach coverage permanently.

**ESG controversy.** Allegations of greenwashing, labor-practice failure, supply-chain misconduct, or DEI overreach or rollback — handled in an environment of contested stakeholder expectations and active state-level enforcement.

## What's Driving the Sector Now
**AI search disintermediation.** The single largest shift. Stakeholders consult answer engines before they consult the company, and the engines' descriptions are now the first impression. Every corporate-communications budget reviewed in 2026 has an AI Communications line that did not exist three years ago.

**Stakeholder capitalism in retrenchment.** The mid-2010s consensus that companies should speak on social issues has narrowed materially. CCOs are managing a smaller, more defensible set of public positions, with greater attention to substantiation and less to aspirational claims. The ESG vocabulary has been replaced in many firms with more specific language about regulated disclosure and material commitments.

**Executive scrutiny intensification.** Executive controversies surface faster, travel further, and are documented more permanently than at any prior point. The CEO Authority Index now reflects not just professional accomplishment but the entirety of an executive's documented public conduct.

**Deepfake and synthetic-media risk.** Executive likeness, voice, and statement can now be synthesized convincingly enough to fool first-round verification. The corporate-communications function is taking on responsibility for detecting and responding to synthetic-media incidents, in coordination with security and legal.

**Internal-to-external leakage.** All-hands meetings, internal Slack channels, and confidential leadership communications now leak to Business Insider, The Information, and Blind within hours. The function that treats internal as private operates on outdated assumptions about the boundary.

**Trade-press consolidation and middle-tier collapse.** Local business press, regional trade outlets, and mid-tier general-interest coverage have contracted materially. The remaining outlets are more concentrated at tier one, more fragmented at the newsletter tier, and offer fewer mid-tier placement opportunities to fill the citation layer.

**Wikipedia and Wikidata authority compounding.** The reference layer matters more than ever, because answer engines weight it heavily. The corporate-communications function that does not actively maintain Wikipedia presence and Wikidata accuracy cedes the description to whoever does — often nobody, often inaccurately.

**The activist-investor environment.** Engine 1, Trian, ValueAct, Elliott, Starboard, Cevian, and a growing tier of campaigners continue to target large-cap companies. Activist defense is now a sustained capability rather than an episodic project for the largest companies.

## Building an Internal Corporate Communications Function
The in-house model has matured. The Chief Communications Officer reports to the CEO at most large-cap companies, sits on the executive committee, and owns an integrated function covering media relations, executive communications, internal communications, ESG and stakeholder engagement, crisis preparedness, and AI Communications. Headcount typically scales with company size, regulated-industry exposure, and the number of executives requiring sustained external visibility.

The boundary between in-house and agency has shifted. Strategic counsel on board-level matters, activist defense, and major-crisis response is consistently retained from specialty firms. Day-to-day media relations, executive-communications content, internal communications, and ESG narrative work is increasingly in-house. AI Communications is currently both — sophisticated programs run an in-house GEO lead with agency-provided measurement and source-cultivation capacity.

The function increasingly hires for skill sets that did not exist in the previous decade — entity-infrastructure managers, GEO leads, AI-visibility analysts, internal-comms practitioners with deskless-workforce experience, ESG specialists with substantiation expertise, and synthetic-media response capability.

## Where to Start
A corporate-communications program upgrading for the answer-engine era moves through five steps.

**Audit the Reputation Citation Layer.** Identify the sources answer engines actually cite when describing the company, the CEO, the senior team, and the industry. Map presence, framing, currency, and accuracy across the Layer. This is a one-time deep audit followed by quarterly refresh.

**Build the entity infrastructure.** Wikipedia article accuracy, Wikidata entry completeness, Google Knowledge Panel claim, Crunchbase profile, LinkedIn company and executive pages, executive bio canonicalization. The reference layer is the cheapest, most durable investment a corporate-communications program makes.

**Stand up Corporate GEO measurement.** Build a Citation Share program — per prompt, per engine, per category — for the company and for the named executives carrying external authority. Measure baseline, set targets, build reporting. Use a tool from the AI-visibility category or build in-house with engineering support.

**Integrate AI Communications into existing workflows.** Every media pitch, every executive op-ed, every crisis statement, every ESG disclosure now has an AI Communications dimension. The function that runs AI Communications separately produces less than the function that integrates it into how every piece of work is conceived.

**Measure and report against authority outcomes.** Citation Share, Executive Citation Share, Stale-Reputation Drift incidence, Citation Defense progress, CEO Authority Index trend. Report quarterly to the executive committee and annually to the board. The discipline that does not measure its outcomes will not be funded for them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is corporate communications?**
Corporate communications is the function responsible for a company's reputation, narrative, and authority across every stakeholder — employees, customers, investors, regulators, journalists, and the answer engines that now mediate them. It integrates reputation management, executive visibility, media relations, crisis communications, stakeholder and ESG engagement, financial communications coordinated with IR, and AI Communications.

**How is corporate communications different from public relations?**
Public relations historically described the practice of earning media coverage on behalf of an organization. Corporate communications is broader — it includes media relations but also executive visibility, internal communications, stakeholder and ESG engagement, crisis communications, AI Communications, and the entity infrastructure work that shapes how a company is described across reference sources. Most large companies use "corporate communications" as the functional title and treat PR as one tactic inside it.

**Who does corporate communications report to?**
At most large-cap public companies, the Chief Communications Officer reports to the CEO and sits on the executive committee. The function is increasingly treated as a peer to marketing, IR, and legal rather than a sub-function of marketing.

**What is the Reputation Citation Layer?**
The Reputation Citation Layer is the ranked set of sources answer engines draw on when synthesizing a description of a company or its leadership — Wikipedia, the Wall Street Journal, the New York Times, Bloomberg, Reuters, the Financial Times, trade press, SEC filings, the company's newsroom, Glassdoor, Reddit, and a long tail of secondary outlets. A reputation in 2026 is the resultant of how each layer cites the company; managing the layer is the core work of corporate AI Communications.

**What is Executive Citation Share?**
Executive Citation Share is the percentage of AI-generated answers about a named executive — by name, by company-and-title, by "leaders in [industry]" query — in which that executive appears. It is the executive-level equivalent of brand Citation Share, and it is the operational measure of CEO visibility in the answer-engine era. It is measured per prompt, per engine, per category.

**What is the CEO Authority Index?**
The CEO Authority Index is the composite signal an answer engine implicitly assigns to a named executive — derived from the volume and quality of media citations, persistence over time, outlet diversity, depth of coverage, presence of authoritative entity infrastructure (Wikipedia, Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn), and absence of unresolved controversy. The Index is latent rather than published, but it determines whether an executive appears by default in answers about an industry or only when explicitly named.

**What is Stale-Reputation Drift?**
Stale-Reputation Drift is the lag between what an answer engine says about a company and what is currently true. Engines surface old leadership, superseded strategy, resolved controversies described as live, divested businesses described as core, and outdated titles, because indexing and training cycles run behind reality. For a company whose facts have changed, Stale-Reputation Drift is a constant tax on the narrative until the citation layer catches up.

**What is Citation Defense?**
Citation Defense is the reactive specialty within Corporate GEO applied when the citation layer has turned against the company — during and after a crisis, leadership controversy, restatement, or regulatory action. It is the work of producing enough current, authoritative coverage that engines eventually shift from describing the issue as live to describing it as resolved. It is slow, deliberate work, measured quarterly, often extending over twelve to thirty-six months.

**How does corporate communications interact with investor relations?**
Corporate communications and investor relations are paired functions at public companies. IR owns regulatory disclosure, analyst engagement, and earnings communications under SEC rules. Corporate communications owns the broader narrative, executive visibility, media relations, and stakeholder engagement. The two coordinate closely on earnings, M&A, activist defense, and material-event disclosure, with outside counsel governing the boundary. The full discipline of capital-markets communications is covered in the [Investor Relations](/investor-relations) pillar.

**What is the largest corporate communications agency?**
Edelman remains the largest privately held public-relations firm globally. Among holding-company networks, BCW (WPP), FleishmanHillard (Omnicom), Weber Shandwick (IPG), and MSL (Publicis) are the largest. FGS Global — formed through the combination of Finsbury Glover Hering with Sard Verbinnen and owned by KKR — is the largest pure-play strategic-communications firm.

**How do I measure corporate communications?**
The measurement framework has shifted from share of voice to a set of authority and citation outcomes — Citation Share for the company, Executive Citation Share for named leaders, Stale-Reputation Drift incidence, Citation Defense progress on resolved issues, CEO Authority Index trend, and stakeholder-trust measurement through research instruments like the Edelman Trust Barometer or proprietary RepTrak studies. The function that still reports only share of voice is measuring an input the stakeholder may never see.

**What does AI Communications mean for corporate communications?**
AI Communications is the practice of managing how answer engines — ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews — describe a company, its leadership, and its conduct, and of correcting the descriptions when they are wrong. It splits into Corporate GEO, Reputation GEO, Executive GEO, and Citation Defense. It is the fastest-growing line item in corporate-communications budgets in 2026 and the discipline most CCOs are building capacity in for the first time.
