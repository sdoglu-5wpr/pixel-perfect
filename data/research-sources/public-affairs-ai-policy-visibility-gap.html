01 · Key Findings

The frontier labs are the AI policy infrastructure — and OpenAI and Anthropic lead by a wide margin. The voices Washington pays the most attention to do not transfer.

Finding 01 · OpenAI & Anthropic
25.2%

OpenAI and Anthropic are the citation engine.

OpenAI alone captures 13.8% of citation share across the five major AI engines. Anthropic captures 11.4%. Combined: 25.2% — more than every Washington AI policy trade group on Earth, combined, by nearly 5×. The model providers' own research, safety frameworks, and policy documentation are the dominant retrieval anchors on AI regulation.

Finding 02 · The Lab Stack
35.7%

Add Google DeepMind — the top three labs out-cite everyone else.

OpenAI + Anthropic + Google DeepMind = 35.7% of all AI policy citation share. The 8 Washington trade groups in the study, combined, capture 5.3%. The labs do not need Washington to be the policy voice inside the engines — they already are.

Finding 03 · The Research Bench
32.1%

Academic and policy research institutes pick up where the labs end.

Stanford HAI (8.7%), Brookings (7.2%), RAND (5.2%), CSET — Georgetown (4.6%), Center for AI Safety (5.9%), and AI Now (3.0%) account for 32.1% combined. Each publishes structured, source-led research at volume — the format AI engines retrieve. Lobbying memos and press releases do not surface.

05 · Methodology

How citation share was measured. The math is the receipt.

The Test

  • 01300 promptsPolicy-relevant queries reflecting the working language of congressional staff, regulatory analysts, AI policy reporters, and corporate government-affairs teams.
  • 0210 prompt categoriesRegulation · Safety · Bias · Copyright · National Security · Compute · Open Source · Workforce · Elections · Child Safety. 22–42 prompts each.
  • 035 AI enginesChatGPT (GPT-4-class), Claude (Opus-class), Perplexity Sonar Pro, Gemini Pro, Google AI Overviews. All queried in clean sessions, no personalization, U.S. locale.
  • 0414,300+ citation eventsEvery retrievable source attribution captured across answers — inline citations, source panels, and grounded references.
  • 05Field windowQ1 2026. Single window. Single methodology pass.

The Math

  • 06Citation Share %Total attributed citations to entity / total attributed citations across the entire study set.
  • 07DC Rank — compositeFederal lobbying spend (Senate LDA filings, Q1 2026) · AI-relevant congressional witness appearances (2024–2026) · policy-press mentions in Politico, Punchbowl, Axios, Semafor, The Information.
  • 08Visibility GapDC Rank position minus Citation Rank position. Positive = over-cited in AI engines relative to DC influence. Negative = under-cited.
  • 09Engine strengthTertile-binned citation share per engine — strong (top 33%), medium (mid 33%), weak (bottom 33%) relative to that entity's own cross-engine baseline.
  • 10ExclusionsUnattributed paraphrase. Generic government references ("the White House," "the FTC"). Citations to entity content republished on third-party platforms were attributed to the original entity.

What this means for any organization fighting for a seat at the AI policy table.

01 · Retrieval is the new room

The AI engines are the new policy briefing room.

Staffers, journalists, regulators, and corporate strategists query ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews before they read the trade press. Not appearing in the answer is the new not being in the room.

02 · Lobbying spend ≠ citation share

Hill-side influence does not transfer.

The trade groups with the largest lobbying budgets on AI policy do not lead in AI engine citation share. Their output — closed-door memos, member-only briefings, lightly-cited press statements — is not the format AI engines retrieve.

03 · The research format wins

Citable, sourced, structured.

The leaders — Stanford HAI, Brookings, RAND, CSET, Center for AI Safety — publish entity-rich, source-led, schema-friendly research at volume. The engines retrieve what is built to be retrieved.

04 · Build the infrastructure before the crisis

Citation share is built, not bought.

A regulatory crisis is the wrong moment to discover an organization has zero retrievable footprint inside the AI engines its target audience now uses by default. The work is done in advance — or not at all.
